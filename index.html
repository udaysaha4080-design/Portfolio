§ 01 — Biography

A mind shaped
by questions,
not answers.

Uday is a researcher at the intersection of genetics, evolutionary biology, and theoretical thought. He is not merely learning biology — he is constructing a personal cosmology of life: how organisms remember across generations, how mutations sculpt populations across millennia, how consciousness might emerge from the molecular choreography of living systems.

"Science, for me, is a form of slow attention — the discipline of noticing what life is actually doing,beneath the noise of what we expect it to do."

His academic roots lie in Genetic Engineering and Biotechnology at Daffodil International University, Dhaka. But his intellectual territory extends far beyond institutional boundaries — into philosophy of biology, systems thinking, microbial ecology, and the strange territory where mathematics meets metaphysics.

He thinks in Bengali and English simultaneously — two registers of a single restless mind that finds home equally in population genetics textbooks and slow cinema, in the equations of Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium and the silence of a Rumi verse at 3 a.m.

§ 07 — Manifesto

On living,
mutating,
and remembering.

I. On the Archive

I study genetics because I cannot accept that life is accidental without also believing that it is, in its accidentality, sublime. Every genome is an archive. Every mutation is a sentence written by time into a manuscript that did not ask to be written. Every generation is a revision — incomplete, imprecise, and irreversible — of a text that began four billion years ago and has no final draft.

"Science is not a collection of facts. It is a practice of sustained wonder, conducted with precision and without sentimentality."
II. On Equilibrium

I am drawn to population genetics because it is honest about uncertainty. The Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium exists only when nothing is happening — no selection, no mutation, no drift, no migration, no nonrandom mating. Real life is the perpetual violation of equilibrium. Real life is always mid-transition, always between states, always becoming something that it has not yet decided to be. I find this comforting. I find this true.

III. On Deep Time

I believe the most important questions in biology are not molecular. They are temporal. How does information persist across death? How does selection know what to select for, if the environment of selection is itself evolving? How does complexity maintain itself at the edge of thermodynamic dissolution, when everything tends toward disorder and yet life, obstinately, tends toward structure?

"Evolution does not optimise — it satisfices, forever, across an infinite and shifting landscape of possible forms. It is not engineering. It is geology."
IV. On Method

I am also a student of cinema, of poetry, of silence. I do not think this is separate from science. Slow cinema teaches patience with duration — the same patience required to think in evolutionary time. Poetry teaches the compression of maximum complexity into minimum language. Philosophy teaches that every answer contains three new questions, which is also, structurally, the shape of a good experiment.

V. On the Project

I am building — slowly, incompletely, with full awareness that I may never finish — a personal theory of life. Not a textbook. Not a career. A way of seeing. A framework for attending, carefully and without flinching, to the extraordinary and still-unexplained fact that matter has learned to contemplate itself.

"আমি মৃত্যুকে ভয় পাই না। আমি ভয় পাই অমনোযোগকে — সেই জীবনকে যা নিজের বিস্ময়কে ভুলে যায়, এবং তারপরও চলতে থাকে, নিষ্প্রাণভাবে।"
